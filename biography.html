Biography

Devanesan Nesiah was born on April 28, 1935 in Jaffna to Kunasekaram Nesiah and his wife Pushpamany Somasundaram. His early years were spent with his three younger siblings at 20 Somasundaram Lane, the house built by his mother’s father, the Canon Somasundaram. K. Nesiah was a member of the Jaffna Youth Congress; many political meetings were held at their house in the decades before independence. Pushpamany taught Devanesan how to play chess; he loved especially to play with his uncles, Peter and George Somasundaram.

Devanesan attended kindergarten at Chundikuli Girls’ College and began at St. John’s College in elementary school. When his father got a teaching post at S. Thomas’s College (Mount Lavinia), the family moved with him to Moor Road, in Wellawatta. At S. Thomas’ he won the Miller maths prize, and was an avid user of the STC library; he was greatly influenced by Abraham Kavoor, his STC maths and religion teacher, who encouraged critical thinking.

For his A-levels, Devanesan did double maths and physics and entered an engineering program at the University of Colombo. Once enrolled he switched to mathematics, which he preferred, but was increasingly alienated from his studies as anti-Tamil sentiment grew in the country, solidified by the Sinhala Only Act. He began organizing in college, traveling the country with his friends in a car borrowed from his father to canvass voters for the LSSP. He neglected his classes and scraped by with a pass. After graduation he returned to his parents, who had moved to Peradeniya, where his father had become a university professor.

At the last possible minute he enrolled in the civil service exams and came first in the island, securing a place in the civil service (despite flubbing the interview). After cadet service in various departments he was appointed Assistant Government Agent in Badulla in his mid-twenties.

In 1964 he married Anita Seevaratnam, who was teaching at St. Thomas’s Prep and living with her family in Bambalapitiya. Soon after their marriage he was appointed Government Agent of Mannar, where their eldest son, Vaseeharan, was born in 1964. His second child, Vasuki, was born in 1968, after his transfer to be GA Batticaloa.

After a few years in the east he was transferred to Colombo, where he hosted a British delegation studying Sri Lanka’s development plans. They were impressed with his work in Mannar and Batticaloa, where he had supported local industries and regional arts traditions, and invited him to come to the famed development studies program at the University of Sussex for an MA. He and Anita moved to Sussex for a year, where they followed the Bangladeshi war of liberation, met the Queen, and visited their siblings in Switzerland and Canada.

When they returned to Colombo they moved to Mount Lavinia with Vasee and Vasuki, in a house they had built on land that had been bombed by the Japanese during World War II. He walked with the children on the beach each night after work at the Ministry of Planning Implementation. During this period he became increasingly involved in competitive chess and had the opportunity to lead the Sri Lankan chess team to international tournaments. He also worked with others in the chess community in helping to build the chess federation and encourage a younger generation of chess players. He also had the opportunity to travel around Eastern Europe and the Soviet bloc with the Christian Peace Conference, which was headquartered in Prague. In 1974 he welcomed his third child, Kumanan.

During this period he encountered many pressures at work, in the MPI and other departments. He refused bribes and compromises and was consequently denied promotions and rewards and frequently relegated to the “pool,” where non-fireable civil servants languished; he was said to have held the record for the longest time spent in the pool. During these periods he played more chess than ever.

The family had moved to Colpetty by this time and could shelter friends who had been displaced in the anti-Tamil riots of 1978. After the burning of the Jaffna Public Library, the TULF pushed the government to accede to some demands for remediation, which included appointing him GA Jaffna. He moved to the government housing in Old Park, a few blocks from his parents’ house. He saw his role as restoring civilian governance, as much as was possible within his remit, and worked to create a sense of local trust and democratic ownership.

After the 1983 pogrom, tens of thousands of displaced Tamils arrived in Jaffna from the south. He was responsible for resettling them and providing relief support, in which he was helped by his wife; she transformed the official residence into a center of Red Cross operations. There was increasing violence in Jaffna too, as the war began, including a bomb that exploded in his office; he refused the government narrative, which made every Jaffna citizen a potential “militant” or “terrorist,” and every time there was a shooting or a bomb he would visit the victim’s family, regardless of who the perpetrator was. This often incorporated fact-finding missions, which meant contradicting official army statements to the international media.

Under some pressure from the central government, in 1983 he decided to take a short-term sabbatical, accepting an offer to study public policy at the Harvard Kennedy School, which became a PhD in Public Administration under the advisory of the Nobel Prize-winning economist Thomas Schelling. He published his dissertation, a comparative study of affirmative action across India, the US, and Malaysia, as a book called Discrimination With Reason?, published by Oxford University Press.

Upon his return to Sri Lanka, he worked as the secretary of various ministries, including transportation, and women’s affairs; in his capacity as Secretary of Environment he represented Sri Lanka at the UN’s 1992 “Earth Summit” in Rio de Janeiro. He retired from official government life in 1995 but continued to participate in official work, including commissions investigating violence in the North and East, on press freedom, on postwar reconciliation, and on public service. He also worked for various think tanks, human rights and democracy-promoting organizations, especially the Marga Institute and the Centre for Policy Analysis, and in various ways stayed involved in politics for many decades. He was awarded the Deshamanya prize, Sri Lanka’s highest civilian honor, in 2017; he returned it in 2018, in protest at then-President Maithripala Sirisena’s illegal dissolution of Parliament.

He is survived by his sisters, Nimala and Pushpadevi (his brother Lanka died in 2013); his three children, Vasee, Vasuki, and Kumanan, and children-in-law, Nanthi and Sayeba; his nieces and nephews, Sashika, Kaushala, Niranjala, Sangeetha, and Nagulan; his grandchildren, Udhayanan, Merhini, Nawal, and Sanjeevi; his great-grandchildren, Sivanandan and Rajan; and many more cousins, nieces, nephews, grandnieces, and grandnephews. He died on June 12, 2026, at Vasee’s home in Colombo.
